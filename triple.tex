\section{Result}\label{result}

\begin{theorem}[]\protect\hypertarget{thm-major}{}\label{thm-major}

Suppose \(X\) is \(n\times m\) and \(R\) and \(C\) are positive
semi-definite of orders \(n\) and \(m\). Define \[
\sigma(X):=\|R^\frac12XC^\frac12\|^2.
\] Suppose \(\tilde X\) is any other \(n\times m\) matrix. Define
\(\tilde G:=R\tilde XC\) and \(\tilde V:=\tilde X-\tilde G\). Then \[
\sigma(X)\leq\sigma(\tilde X)+\lambda\|X-\tilde V\|^2-\lambda^{-1}\|\tilde G\|^2
\] where \(\lambda\) is larger than or equal to the largest eigenvalue
of \(R\otimes C\) (the product of the largest eigenvalues of \(R\) and
\(C\)).

\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If \(x:=\text{vec}(X)\) then, using the Kronecker Product,
\(\sigma(X)=x'(C\otimes R)x\). Write \(x=\tilde x+(x-\tilde x)\). Then
\begin{multline}
\sigma(x)=\sigma(\tilde x)+2(x-\tilde x)'(C\otimes R)\tilde x+(x-\tilde x)'(C\otimes R)(x-\tilde x)'\leq\\\sigma(\tilde x)+2(x-\tilde x)'(C\otimes R)\tilde x+\lambda(x-\tilde x)'(x-\tilde x).
\end{multline} Completing the square, rearranging terms, and using
\(\text{vec}(R\tilde XC)=(C\otimes R)\tilde x\) gives the required
result.
\end{proof}

Now suppose \(X=W\star(Y-Z)\) and \(\tilde X=W\star(Y-\tilde Z)\), with
a parametrization of \(Z\) similar to that in WALS (De Leeuw and
Graffelman (\citeproc{ref-deleeuw_graffelman_E_22e}{2022})). Thus
\begin{equation}
z_{ij}=\mu+r_i+c_j+\sum_{s=1}^pa_{is}b_{js}.
\end{equation} For majorization to work it is necessary that
\(\tilde Z\) is of the same form. Using the majorization in
Theorem~\ref{thm-major} means that in each step we have to minimize (or
at least decrease) the weighted sum \begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^mw_{ij}^2((y_{ij}-z_{ij})-\tilde v_{ij}/w_{ij})^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m w_{ij}^2(\tilde y_{ij}-z_{ij})^2
\end{equation} with \begin{equation}
\tilde y_{ij}:=y_{ij}-\frac{\tilde v_{ij}}{w_{ij}}.
\end{equation} over \(Z\), and we know how to do this.

\section{Decomposition}\label{decomposition}

And now for something completely different. The problem we study in this
section is to approximate a \(n\times m\) matrix \(X\) by a matrix \(Y\)
with \begin{equation}
y_{ij}=\mu+\alpha_i+\beta_j+\gamma_{ij}
\end{equation} where \begin{align}
&\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i=0,\\
&\sum_{j=1}^m \beta_j=0,\\
&\sum_{i=1}^n \gamma_{ij}=0\qquad\forall j,\\
&\sum_{j=1}^m \gamma_{ij}=0\qquad\forall i.
\end{align}

\subsection{Unweighted Least Squares}\label{unweighted-least-squares}

Suppose \(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\) is the space of all \(n\times m\)
matrices, with the inner product \(\langle X,Y\rangle=\text{tr}\ X'Y\)
and the corresponding squared norm \(\|X\|^2=\text{tr}\ X'X\). We define
four subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\).

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_0\) of all constant matrices.
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_r\) of all matrices \(ae'\) with \(a\)
  centered.
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_c\) of all matrices \(eb'\) with \(b\)
  centered.
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_d\) of all doubly-centered matrices.
\end{enumerate}

These four subspaces are orthogonal and span \(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\).
Each \(n\times m\) matrix \(X\) can be written as \(X=X_0+X_r+X_c+X_d\),
where each of the four orthogonal components is the least squares
projection of \(X\) on the corresponding subspace.

We use the rank one symmetric projectors \begin{align}
P&=\frac{1}{n}e_ne_n',\\
Q&=\frac{1}{m}e_me_m'.
\end{align} Now the parameter estimates are \begin{align}
\mu&=PXQ,\label{eq-par1}\\
\alpha&=(I-P)Xe_m/m,\label{eq-par2}\\
\beta&=(I-Q)X'e_n/n,\label{eq-par3}\\
\Gamma&=(I-P)X(I-Q).\label{eq-par4}
\end{align} Thus \(\alpha\) are the row averages in deviations from
their mean, and \(\beta\) the column averages in deviations from their
mean, \(\mu\) is the overall mean of \(X\), and \(\Gamma\) is the
doubly-centered version of \(X\).

It follows that \begin{align}
X_0&=\mu ee'=PXQ,\\
X_r&=ae'=(I-P)XQ,\\
X_c&=eb'=PX(I-Q),\\
X_d&=Y=(I-P)X(I-Q).
\end{align} Also \[
\|X-Y\|^2=\|X_0-Y_0\|^2+\|X_r-Y_r\|^2+\|X_c-Y_c\|^2+\|X_d-Y_d\|^2
\] As a consequence, minimizing \(\|X-Y\|^2\) over \(Y\) while
requiring, for example, \(Y_c=0\) has the solution \(Y=X_0+X_r+X_d\). If
we require that the row parameters \(a\) are increasing we minimize
\(\|\hat a-a\|^2\) over \(a_1\leq\cdots\leq a_n\), with \(\hat a\) the
centered row-means. The estimateds of \(Y_0\), \(Y_c\), and \(Y_d\)
remain the same., If we require that \(Y\) is of rank \(p<min(n,m)\) we
just compute the low-rank least squares approximation of the doubly
centered matrix \(X_d\).

\subsection{Kronecker Weighting}\label{kronecker-weighting}

The next step is to generalize these results to the inner product
\(\langle X,Y\rangle=\text{tr}\ RXCY'\) on \(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\)
with squared norm \(\|X\|^2=\text{tr}\ RXCX'\). Suppose \(R\) and \(C\)
are both positive definite. We call this \emph{Kronecker Weighting}.

Now \begin{align}
\langle \alpha e',ee'\rangle&=\text{tr}\ R\alpha e'Cee'=e'Ce\times e'R\alpha ,\\
\langle \alpha e',e\beta'\rangle&=\text{tr}\ R\alpha e'C\beta e'=e'R\alpha \times e'C\beta,\\
\langle \alpha e',Y\rangle&=\text{tr}\ R\alpha e'CY'=\alpha 'RYCe.
\end{align} Thus our four orthogonal subspaces are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_0\) of all constant matrices \(\mu ee'\).
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_r\) of all matrices \(\alpha e'\) with
  \(\alpha 'Re=0\).
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_c\) of all matrices \(e\beta'\) with
  \(\beta'Ce=0\).
\item
  The subspace \(\mathbb{L}_d\) of all matrices \(Y\) with \(YCe=0\) and
  \(Y'Re=0\).
\end{enumerate}

We now compute projections in the weighted Kronecker metric on these
subspaces. For this we use idempotent, asymmetric rank-one projectors.
\begin{align}
P&=\frac{ee'R}{e'Re},\\
Q&=\frac{Cee'}{e'Ce}
\end{align} and their complements \(I-P\) and \(I-Q\). Note that
\(P^2=P\) and \(Q^2=Q\) so that \(P(I-P)=0\) and \(Q(I-Q)=0\).

The solution for the parameters are \begin{align}
\hat\mu&=PXQ,\\
\hat\alpha&=(I-P)XCe/e'Ce,\\
\hat\beta'&=e'RX(I-Q)/e'Re,\\
\hat Y&=(I-P)X(I-Q).
\end{align} so that \begin{align}
\hat\alpha e'&=(I-P)XQ,\\
e\hat\beta'&=PX(I-Q),
\end{align} and \begin{equation}
X=\hat\mu+\hat\alpha e'+e\hat\beta'+\hat Y
\end{equation}

\subsection{Precision Weights}\label{precision-weights}

There are inner products on matrix space for which the previous results
are not all true. Consider, for example, the inner product \[
\langle X,Y\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m w_{ij}x_{ij}y_{ij}
\] For this inner product \begin{align}
<\alpha e',ee'>&=\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i\sum_{j=1}^m w_{ij},\\
<\alpha e',e\beta'>&=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m w_{ij}\alpha_i\beta_j
\end{align} The second inner product is zero for arbitrary \(\alpha\)
and \(\beta\) if and only if \(W\) is zero, which is not a very
interesting situation. The projection \(P\) of \(X\) on \(\alpha e'\) is
\(x_re'\), with \(x_r\) the vector of weighted row means in deviations
from the total mean. Symmetrically \(Q\) is \(ex_c'\), using the
weighted column means.

Trivially it is always true that \[
X=PXQ+(I-P)XQ+PX(I-Q)+(I-P)X(I-Q)
\] For this \(P\) and \(Q\) do not even have to be projectors.
